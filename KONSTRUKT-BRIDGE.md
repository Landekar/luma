## Shared Design Principles

### 1. Virtual Studio Principle
**The single most important insight across both platforms.**

A platform only becomes a venue people return to when users can *create* inside it, not just consume. Libraries don't have retention. Studios do.

- Museums are free. Studios are paid.
- Pinterest is free. Figma is paid.
- Wikipedia is free. Notion is paid.

**Coalesce currently:** A museum (genre library). Needs: Art Direction Editor (Phase 2).
**Obraz currently:** A museum (knowledge graph). Needs: Своя Среда personal canvas (Phase 3).

Neither editor needs to be complex at launch. Minimum viable: a page where a user creates something using the platform's curated content as raw material. Save it. View it. That transforms the product.

### 2. Hard Editorial First
**The sequencing principle. Non-negotiable.**

The curator builds the foundation first — curated, controlled, opinionated. Only after the foundation is solid enough to build on do you open the studio for user agency.

You cannot give users agency inside an empty or unstable foundation. They have nothing to work with.

```
Phase 1: Hard editorial content (Timur curates)
Phase 2: User workspace (users create within the system)
Phase 3: Community publishing (users share — with quality gate)
```

This principle applies identically to both Coalesce and Obraz.

### 3. Content Coverage: Follow the Heat
No systematic coverage plan. The editorial instinct — what Timur keeps bumping into on Pinterest, YouTube, at 2am — IS the roadmap.

Pinterest clusters images by visual similarity. Timur clusters them by *meaning*. That gap is the product.

## Where AudioViz Belongs

**AudioViz (Obraz Labs)** — Vector Keyboard, Modal Mixer, Rhythm Generator, Tone Setter

These tools are interactive demonstrations of the same thesis both platforms are built on: sound and visual correspond. They prove the theory with your hands.

**Current home:** Obraz Labs (R&D)
**Final home:** Coalesce Lab section (Phase 2)

The Vector Keyboard showing interval classes mapped to color/shape perception belongs *inside* Coalesce because:
- Coalesce's genre pages say "Gothic sounds like this." The Vector Keyboard shows *why*.
- It's the proof layer that makes Coalesce intellectually defensible, not just aesthetically compelling.
- Obraz teaches the theory. Coalesce uses it. The Lab is where they meet.

**Obraz's Simulations module** is the archive and teaching context for these tools.
**Coalesce's Lab section** is the live, integrated, contextual use of them.

## The Manifesto (Coalesce)

*Written 2026-03-16 — to be kept as founding context.*

Midjourney launched with 11 people, no VC, no website — just a Discord bot. David Holz wasn't an AI researcher. He was an interface designer who understood experience. The AI was the engine; the vision was about how people interact with creativity.

Coalesce's parallel:
- Solo founder, bootstrapped, no funding
- The competitive advantage isn't technical — it's editorial and experiential
- The people with the "real skills" (3D modelers, concept artists, composers) need someone to organize where it all goes together
- Nobody teaches you how to make all of those things into one coherent world. That's Coalesce.

Schools teach you to model. YouTube teaches you to paint. Spotify gives you music. Pinterest gives you images. Nobody teaches you how all of those things become one coherent world. Coalesce does.

**Starting scope: video games.** Concrete, defensible, deeply underserved by existing tools. Every genre has game analogues. Every game has an art direction. The audience is specific, passionate, and professional.

## The "Digital Garden" Problem

Both Coalesce and Obraz currently feel like "digital gardens" — beautiful, curated, but passive. Something you visit, not somewhere you live.

The diagnosis is identical for both:
- Beautiful content ✓
- Editorial voice ✓
- No user action ✗
- No reason to return daily ✗

The fix is identical for both: **a personal workspace where the user creates something using the platform's knowledge as raw material.**

Until that feature exists in each platform, both will feel like a hobbyist project — something given away for free. That's not a failure of vision. It's a sequencing issue. The editor comes after the content. The studio is built on top of the museum.
